Catalytic nitric oxide producing interfaces from endogenous and synthetic nitrosothiols.

Blood contains nitric oxide (NO) precursors in the form of S-nitrosothiols (RSNOs) and nitrites. The decomposition of RSNOs produces thiyl radicals and NO as a product. Catalysts such as metal ions are known to aid in the process and increase NO generation from RSNOs. We investigate if elemental powders within a polymer matrix co-doped with synthetic RSNOs can catalytically increase NO surface fluxes from the polymer. In addition, these polymer-metal composites can also generate NO from endogenous RSNOs, simultaneously. Biomimetic metalloenzymes are also synthesized to target the alternative sources of NO that are present within the blood. We translate NO generation technology towards the development of medical device surface designs. Thus, helping reduce the risk of infection or thrombosis that are commonly associated with medical device surfaces.

Combination of highly hydrophobic or superhydrophobic anti-fouling biomedical surfaces with NO

Inactivation or prevention of biomolecule adhesion on biomedical surfaces is widely explored. By modulating, surfaces can be rendered highly hydrophobic (HH) or superhydrophobic (SH) and are generally known as non-fouling surfaces. Often, they are inspired by nature, for example, the self- cleaning lotus leaf. Although HH/SH surfaces can minimize bacterial adhesion, it does not affect the viability of adhered bacteria. With time, the accumulation of bacteria can result in the loss of HH/SH character. In such scenarios, bioactive agents such as NO or metal ions can destroy adhered bacteria.

Antimicrobial peptide immobilization on polymer surface and nanomaterials for enhanced antibacterial applications

Antimicrobial peptides are a part of the innate immune response produced to inhibit microbial pathogens. Nisin is one such polycyclic cationic peptide that is FDA approved and classified as GRAS (Generally Regarded as Safe). Nisin is routinely used in the food industry for coating antimicrobial packaging materials. We explore a clinical aspect of nisin and its immobilization on the surface bulk polymer and nanomaterials.

Repurposing natural polymers as nitric oxide delivery vehicles

Natural polymers such as chitosan, silk, etc., are biocompatible and are used for various biomedical applications. We utilize these natural polymers to synthesis delivery vehicles for NO that can potentially have a therapeutic effect for a wide array of applications.
